    9. Summary measures

    Summary measures

    We have seen that categorical data can be displayed graphically with a bar chart.

    An alternative display is a pie chart but this restricts the amount of detail included and is less appropriate for making comparisons than a clustered bar chart.

    Measures of Central Tendency

    The most useful summary measure of a categorical variable is the percentage, but with quantitative variables we can do more. Since quantitative data generally take several distinct values, their distribution is often summarised in terms of:

    • its location (or central tendency)
    • its spread (or variation)

    The three commonly used measures of location are the mean, the median and the mode.

    Each has its own advantages and disadvantages which depend on the type of data being summarised.

    Mean

    The mean is the conventional average. It is the sum of the observations divided by the number of observations.

    If we say each of n observations has a value xi then the mean value x will be:

    χ =

    ∑xi

     

      n  


    Example of a Mean

    If we take the weights in kilograms of 5 medical students:

    53.5, 65.0, 74.2, 55.6, 51.2

    Then the mean weight is:

    (53.5 + 65.0 + 74.2 + 55.6 + 51.2) / 5 = 59.9 kg

    Median

    The median is the middlemost score. That is, it is the score that represents the exact halfway point through the data set. The median score divides the set of data into two equal halves. Half of the scores falls below the median, and half falls above it. The example below shows how we find the median of an odd number of data values.

    Returning to the student's weights that we used previously, if we put the weights in order:

    51.2, 53.5, 55.6, 65.0, 74.2

    The median is the middle value, so:

    Median = 55.6

    The  median is not sensitive to extreme values

    51.2, 53.5, 55.6, 65.0, 174.2

    For example, if 74.2 became 174.2, the median would remain the same, but the mean would change from 59.9 kg to 79.9 kg .

    Suppose however that you had an even number of data values. How could you find the middle value?

    When there is an even number of data values, there is no single middle value. In this case the median is defined as the mean of the central pair of values.

    An extra value has been added to the data below.

    Student's weights (kg):

    51.2, 53.5, 55.6, 61.4, 65.0, 74.2

    Median of an even number of data values

    In this example the central values are 55.6 and 61.4. Therefore the median is the mean of these values:

    55.6 + 61.4

    = 58.5

    Mode

    The mode is the most frequently occurring score in a set of scores.

    82 82 83 83 84 85 86 87 87 87 88 90 95 99 99

    The mode of the above set of numbers is 87 because it appears three times — more than any other number in the set.

    82 83 84 86 87 88 88 89 90 91 91 92 94 97 98

    There are two modes above. The numbers 88 and 91 both appear twice. This is a

    bi-modal (two modes) data set.

    82 83 84 86 87 88 89 90 91 92 93 94 95 96 97

    There is no mode for this distribution. No score occurs more frequently than any other. The mode is the most frequent score in a set of data.

    Properties of the Mean, Median & Mode

    • The mean is sensitive to outliers; the others are not.
    • The mode may be affected by small changes in the data; the others are not.
    • The mode and median may be found graphically.
    • All three measures of location are equal for a symmetric distribution; in a skewed distribution they differ (see below).

    When a distribution is a normal ("bell-shaped") curve, all three measures of central tendency have the same value. If a distribution is skewed, the three measures differ in a predictable way. The mode will always equal the highest frequency value. The mean moves away from the mode

    in the direction of the skew. (Extreme scores pull the mean toward them).

    In the right or positively  skewed distribution the mean is greater than the mode.

    In the left  or negatively skewed distribution the mean is smaller than the mode. The median is always between the mode and the  mean.

    Which one should I use?

    For statistical analysis and inference, the mean is more often used. The mean is preferable because it includes all the information contained in the data, that is every observation and every data value. The median uses every observation, but only the ranks of values.

    Usually for skewed distributions (those with outliers) the median is considered a better measure of location than the mean. The mode is rarely used.

    We have defined three measures of central tendency -- the mode, median, and mean -- and established the prominence of the mean. Now we turn to the second essential characteristic of scores -- variability.

     

    Measures of Variation

    If we look at a set of quantitative data displayed as a frequency distribution or a graph, it is easy to see if observations are widely spread from the mean or clustered around it. It is usually necessary to describe this variability as a numerical value. A measure of the spread of the data, together with the mean, provides an informative but brief summary of a data set.

    There are 3 main ways to summarise the variability of a set of data.

    • give the range of all values
    • calculate the interquartile range of  the data
    • calculate a single numerical measure of the dispersion around the mean: the standard deviation

    Each approach has its own advantages but the latter is most useful in statistical calculation. However, let's first consider the range and interquartile range.

    Range

    It is the difference between the highest and lowest values. Often reported along with the minimum and maximum values and extremely prone to distortion even with few present outliers. It is more useful as an alarm: extreme values indicate potential data import error.

    In 18 students the weight was calculated in kilograms:

    54.6, 56.4, 57.2, 64.6, 67.7, 68.1, 68.7, 69.4, 72.3, 74.8, 76.2, 77.2, 79.1, 83.3, 85.5, 88.5, 88.5, 88.9

    The range will be: 88.9 - 54.6 = 34.3.

    Interquartile range (IQR)

    The IQR is the difference between the upper (Q3) and lower quartiles (Q1) for the values of a variable.

    Now, there is a question: what is the quartile? It is one of the four divisions of the values of a ranked variable which are grouped into four equal (in size) parts.

    Find the median of the variable: it divides the data into 2 groups (high and low). Call this Q2. Now find the median of the low group. Call this Q1. Find the median of the high group and it shall be Q3.

    To wrap everything up, IQR = Q3 - Q1

    It might seem as too much trouble for little use, but it proves particularly useful when drawing box-and-whisker plots.

    Using the same weight data as above, the IQR should be calculated as follows:

    54.6, 56.4, 57.2, 64.6, 67.7, 68.1, 68.7, 69.4, 72.3, 74.8, 76.2, 77.2, 79.1, 83.3, 85.5, 88.5, 88.5, 88.9

    Q2 = median =  73.55
    Q2 divides the data into two sets:
    A lower one including 54.6, 56.4, 57.2, 64.6, 67.7, 68.1, 68.7, 69.4, 72.3 with median 67.7 = Q1 (25%) and
    a higher one including 74.8, 76.2, 77.2, 79.1, 83.3, 85.5, 88.5, 88.5, 88.9 with median 83.3 = Q3 (75%)

    IQR = Q3 - Q1 = 83.3 - 67.7 = 15.6
    The range of the values lying into the middle 50% of the data recorded is 15.6

    Standard deviation

    The most common way of quantifying the variability of a distribution is to calculate its standard deviation. This method uses all the observations, by accounting for all deviations from the mean. By deviations we mean the differences between each observation and the mean.

    The problem with the variance is that it is squared, and so it is not in the same units as the original data. In our example of students' heights, the variance is in units of square metres, which is a unit of area, not height.

    If we take the square root of the variance we get a measure of variability in the same units as the raw data. This quantity is called the standard deviation and tells us the average distance of all the observations in a dataset from the mean

    Standard Deviation = √(0.01) = 0.1

    Which one should I use?

    For statistical analysis and inference, the standard deviation is more often used and is preferable because it includes all the information contained in the data, that is every observation and every data value.

    Usually for skewed distributions (those with outliers) the interquartile range is considered a better measure of variation than the standard deviation. The range is rarely used since it tells us nothing of the dispersion of observations between the maximum and minimum values.

    To summarize

    As appropriate descriptive statistics, the mean and the standard deviation are used when the data are normally distributed, while the median and interquartile range are used when the data are not normally distributed.
