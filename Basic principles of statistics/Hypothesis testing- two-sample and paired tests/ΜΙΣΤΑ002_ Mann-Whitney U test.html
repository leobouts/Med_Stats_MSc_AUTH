    Mann-Whitney U test


    The Mann-Whitney U test is used to compare two unrelated, or independent, samples and is often considered the nonparametric alternative to the two-sample t-test when there is violation of normality or for small sample sizes.

    In this test the two samples are combined and rank ordered together. The strategy is to determine if the values from the two samples are randomly mixed in the rank ordering or if they are clustered at opposite ends when combined. A random rank order would mean that the two samples are not different, while a cluster of one sample's values would indicate a difference between them.     Assumptions

    • random samples have been drawn from the respective populations or the participants have been randomly assigned to two conditions

    • each measurement or observation must correspond to a different participant. In statistical terms, there is independence within groups and mutual independence between groups

    • there must be at least 4 observations in each group - otherwise the test cannot give a significant result irrespective of the difference between the two populations

    When the difference in the medians can be expressed in a null and alternative hypothesis?

    If the distributions of the two groups have similar shapes (as in the below graph), the Mann-Whitney U test can be used to determine whether there are differences in the medians in the two groups.


    Steps for conducting the test

    Step 1. Define the null and alternative hypotheses under study

    H0:  the two groups come from the same population (md1=md2).

    H1:  the two groups do not come from the same population (md1≠.md2)

    Tied observations are assigned the mean of the ranks that would have received if they had not been tied.

    Step 3. Calculate the p value 

    Again, an old fashioned way is to compare the U statistic (the smaller of U1 and U2) with the critical values found at Mann-Whitney tables at the desirable level of significance (usually a=0.05), for a two-tailed test and for sample size of the two groups n1 and n2. This method is rarely used anymore.

    In practice we usually conduct the Mann-Whitney U test using statistical software (e.g. R, SPSS) that automatically provide the p-value.

    Dealing with ties

    If there is a tie, all of the scores that tie receive the average rank of that set of scores (i.e.  there is an observation with score 6 in each group. They each receive rank (4 + 5)/2 = 4.5).
