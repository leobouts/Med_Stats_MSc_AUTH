    Two-sample t-test (Student’s t-test)

     

    Two sample t-test can be used if we have two independent (unrelated) groups eg. males-females, unmatched case-controls, treatment-non treatment and one quantitative variable of interest. For example, we may wish to compare the age in males and females or the weights in two groups of children, each child being randomly allocated to receive either a dietary supplement or placebo.

    Assumptions

    • in the population, the variable is normally distributed in each group (if total sample size less than 25 otherwise this is less important)
    • the underlying variances in the two groups are similar

    Step 3. Calculate the p value from the t-distribution

    The corresponding p-value for the t statistic can be calculated as the cumulative probability Pr(T>|t|) for a variable T that follows a t distribution with n1+n2-2 degrees of freedom. H0 is rejected if the p-value is less than the significance level alpha (e.g. 0.05).

    An old fashioned way for testing H0 would be to compare the calculated t statistic with the corresponding critical value at level α of the t distribution. This is rarely used in the computers era, if ever. 

    In practice, after calculating the t statistic, one may resort to online calculators of the t-distribution (e.g. http://stattrek.com/online-calculator/t-distribution.aspx) in order to get the cumulative probability that T<t. Then, the p-value is 2*(1-Pr(T<t)). Statistical software (e.g. R, SPSS) automatically provide the p-value.
