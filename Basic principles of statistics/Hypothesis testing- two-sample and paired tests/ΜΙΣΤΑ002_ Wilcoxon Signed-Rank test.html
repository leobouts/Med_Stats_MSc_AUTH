    9. Wilcoxon Signed-Rank test

    Wilcoxon Signed-Rank test

    The Wilcoxon signed-rank test, which makes use of the sign and the magnitude of the rank of the differences between pairs of measurements, provides an nonparametric alternative test to the paired t-test when the population distribution of the differences is non-normal or for small sample size.

    Assumptions

    • data are paired and the differences come from the same population
    • each pair is chosen randomly and independent

    • there must be at least 6 pairs of observations - otherwise the test cannot give a significant result irrespective of the difference between the two populations

    The assumption of symmetry

    If you are testing the null hypothesis that the median of the paired differences is zero, then the paired differences must all come from a symmetrical distribution. Note that we do not have to assume that the distributions of the original populations are symmetrical - two very positively skewed distributions that differ only by location will produce a set of paired differences that are symmetrical.


    Steps for conducting the test

    Step 1. Define the null and alternative hypotheses under study

    H0: the median difference is equal to zero (md=0).

    H1: the median difference is not equal to zero (md≠0)

    Step 2. Calculate the value of the test statistic specific to H0

    The following guidelines should be adhered to when ranking the difference scores, Xbefore-Xafter :

    • the absolute values of the difference scores are listed in an ascending order and are ranked
    • any difference score that equals zero is not ranked
    • when there are tied scores present in the data, the average of the ranks involved is assigned to all scores tied for a given rank

    The sum of the ranks of the positive differences (T+) and the sum of the ranks of the negative differences (T−) are calculated. The absolute value of the smaller of the two sum of ranks is defined as the Wilcoxon T test statistic.

     

    Step 3. Calculate the p value

    Again, the old fashioned way is to compare the Wilcoxon T statistic  with the critical values found at tables at the desirable level of significance (usually a=0.05). This method is rarely used anymore.

    In practice we usually conduct the Wilcoxon signed rank test using statistical software (e.g. R, SPSS) that automatically provides the p-value. 

    Step 4. Interpretation

    Interpretation of the results.

    Worked Example

    A study was run to evaluate the effectiveness of an exercise program in reducing systolic blood pressure. A total of 9 patients enrolled in the study, and their systolic blood pressure was measured. Each patient then participates in an exercise training program where they learned proper techniques and execution of a series of exercises. Patients were instructed to do the exercise program 3 times per week for 6 weeks. After 6 weeks, systolic blood pressures was again measured. The data are shown below.

     

    Step 1. Define the null and alternative hypotheses under study

    H0: the median difference is equal to zero.

    H1: the median difference scores is not equal to zero

     

    Step 2. Calculate the value of the test statistic specific to H0

    • calculate the differences, Systolic blood pressure before-Systolic blood pressure after. The difference of the 9th patient equals to zero and should be discarded.
    • order the remaining eight differences in terms of their absolute magnitude. 
    • a progressive rank is assigned to each ordered non-zero difference from 1 to 8
    •  there are two differences of score 8. These are the second and the third ordered absolute difference values, thus their rank is given by (2+3)/2=2.5
    • each rank corrected for ties is signed according to the sign of the difference

    The sum of the ranks of the positive differences is T+= 2.5+2.5+4+5+6+7+8=35 (Mean Rank=35/7=5) and the sum of the ranks of the negative differences is T−=1 (Mean Rank=1). The Wilcoxon T test statistic is T=1 .

    There is significant evidence at a=0.05 that the systolic blood pressure levels before the exercise program (median (IQR) = 153 (130, 160) ) are higher than after the exercise program (median (IQR)=125 (120, 133)), (p=0.020).
