    Paired t-test


    Paired t-test can be used if we have two samples that are related to each other and one quantitative variable of interest. Examples of paired study designs are:

    • measurements collected before and after an intervention in an experimental study
    •  twins, husbands and wives, brothers and sisters
    • matched cases and controls
    • a cross-over trial in which each patient has two measurements on the variable, one while taking active treatment and one while taking placebo

    A paired t-test is used to assess whether the mean of the differences between the two related measurements, x and y, is significantly different from zero.

     

    Assumptions

    In the population of interest, the differences between the pairs of measurements are normally distributed.

    Notation

    Because of the paired nature of the data, the two samples must be of the same size, n.     Steps for conducting the test

    Step 1. Define the null and alternative hypotheses under study

    H0: the mean difference in the population equals zero (μd=0) .

    H1: the mean difference in the population does not equal zero (μd≠0).

    Under the null hypothesis, the t statistic follows the t-distribution with n-1 degrees of freedom (d.f.).


     Step 3. Calculate the p value from the t-distribution

    The corresponding p-value for the t statistic can be calculated as the cumulative probability Pr(T>|t|) for a variable T that follows a t distribution with n-1 degrees of freedom. H0 is rejected if the p-value is less than the significance level alpha (e.g. 0.05).

    An old fashioned way for testing H0 would be to compare the calculated t statistic with the corresponding critical value at level alpha of the t distribution. This is rarely used in the computers era, if ever. 

    In practice, after calculating the t statistic, one may resort to online calculators of the t-distribution (e.g. http://stattrek.com/online-calculator/t-distribution.aspx) in order to get the cumulative probability that T<t. Then, the p-value is 2*(1-Pr(T<t)). Statistical software (e.g. R, SPSS) automatically provide the p-value.
