    9. One-way analysis of variance (ANOVA)

    One-way analysis of variance (ANOVA)

    The one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) is used to determine whether there are any significant differences between the means of two or more independent (unrelated) groups. Although we could perform tests to compare the averages in each pair of groups, the high Type I error rate, resulting from the large number of comparisons, means that we may draw incorrect conclusions. Therefore, we carry out a single global test to determine whether the averages differ in any groups.

    Rationale

    The one-way analysis of variance separates the total variability in the data into that which can be attributed to differences between the individuals from the different groups (the between-group variation), and to the random variation between the individuals within each group (the within-group variation, sometimes called unexplained, error or residual variation).

    These components of variation are measured using variances, hence the name analysis of variance (ANOVA). Under the null hypothesis that the group means are the same, the between-group variance will be similar to the within group variance. If, however, there are differences between the groups, then the between-group variance will be larger than the within-group variance. The F ratio, which is the test statistic for ANOVA, is obtained by deviding between-group variability by within-group variability.

     

    Assumptions

    • we have k independent samples, each sample should be randomly selected from the population it represents
    • the dependent variable should be approximately normally distributed for each category of the independent variable
    • the variances of the k underlying populations represented by the k samples are equal to one another (homogeneity of variance)

    Steps for conducting the test

    Step 1. Define the null and alternative hypotheses under study

    H0:  all group means in the population are equal (μi=μj, i,j=1,2,3,...)

    H1:  at least one group mean in the population differs from the others (μi≠μj, i,j=1,2,3,...)

    Each sum of square (SSt, SSb, SSw) has  different degrees of freedom associated with it.     Equivalency of one-way ANOVA and two-sample t-test

    Data for an experiment involving k = 2 independent groups can be evaluated with either an  one-way analysis of variance or a two-sapmle t-test. The two-sample t-test is a special case of ANOVA. When both of the aforementioned tests are employed to evaluate the same set of data, they will yield the same result.     Step 3. Calculate the p-value from the corresponding F-distribution

    The corresponding p-value for the F statistic can be calculated as the cumulative probability Pr(F>F) for a variable F that follows an F-distribution with dfb and dfw degrees of freedom. H0 is rejected if the p-value is less than the significance level alpha (e.g. 0.05).

    An equivalent, old fashioned way for testing H0 would be to compare the F statistic with the critical values of the F-distribution at the desirable level of significance and for the degree of freedoms of the numerator (dfb) and denominator (dfw). If  (which means that p-value<0.05) then Ho is rejected. This is rarely used in the computers era though. 

    In practice, after calculating the F statistic, one may resort to online calculators of the F-distribution (e.g. http://stattrek.com/online-calculator/f-distribution.aspx)  in order to get the cumulative probability that F<F. Then, the p-value is 1-Pr(F<F). Statistical software (e.g. R, SPSS) automatically provides the p-value. 

    Step 4. Interpretation

    Interpretation of the results. If we obtain a significant result at this initial stage, we may consider performing specific pairwise post-hoc comparisons. We can use one of a number of special tests devised for this purpose (e.g. Bonferroni, Fisher's Least Significant Differences [LSD], Tukey, Scheffé’s, Duncan’s).
