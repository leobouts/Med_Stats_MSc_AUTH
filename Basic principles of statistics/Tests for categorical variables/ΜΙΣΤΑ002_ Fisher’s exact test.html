    Fisher’s exact test

    Fisher's exact test is a statistical test used to determine if there are non-random associations between two categorical variables. When the expected numbers are very small (expected frequencies less than 1 or more than 20% of the expected frequencies less than 5) the chi-square test is not a good approximation and the Fisher's exact test should be used instead.

    For hand calculations, the test is only feasible in the case of a 2 × 2 contingency table. However, the principle of the test can be extended to the general case of an r × c table, and some statistical packages provide a calculation (sometimes using a Monte Carlo method to obtain an approximation) for the more general case.

    Worked Example

    The  contingency table below shows the results from a study to compare two treatment regimens for controlling bleeding in hemophiliacs undergoing surgery. Only one (7.7%) of the 13 hemophiliacs given treatment regimen A suffered bleeding complications, compared to three (25%) of the 12 given regimen B. These numbers are too small for the chi-squared test to be valid; the overall total, 25, is relatively small, and the smallest expected value, (12x4)/25=1.92 (complications with regimen B ), is less than 5. The exact test is therefore indicated.


    Step 1. Define the null and alternative hypotheses under study

    H0: There is no association between the treatment regimens and the bleeding complications.

    H1: There is a association between the treatment regimens and the bleeding complications.
