    McNemar test

    Paired categorical measurements taken from the same participants on two occasions (before-after studies, cross-over trials) or matched categorical data collected in matched case – control studies must be analyzed using tests for correlated data. The measurements collected in these types of study designs are not independent and therefore chi-square test cannot be used because the assumptions would be violated. In this situation, McNemar’s test, for 2x2 tables, is used to assess whether there is a significant change in proportions over time for paired data or whether there is a significant difference in proportions between matched cases and controls. In this type of analysis, the outcome of interest is the within person changes or the within-pair differences and there are no explanatory variables. Examples for McNemar’s test study designs are shown in the following 2x2 cross-tabulation tables:

    Assumptions
    • The sample data have been randomly selected and consist of matched pairs.
    •  Large samples and b+c>25 (in the case that b+c<25, the exact binomial probability should be computed).



    Steps for conducting the test

    Step 1. Define the null and alternative hypotheses under study

    H0: The two marginal probabilities for each outcome are the same, pa + pb = pa + pc  and pc + pd = pb + pd which means that  pb = pc. 

    H1: The two marginal probabilities for each outcome are not the same, pa + pb ≠pa + pc  and pc + pd ≠ pb + pd which means that  pb≠ pc.


    The null hypothesis in practice

    • The null hypothesis for before-after data is that the proportion of subjects with the characteristic is the same before and after treatment pa+b= pa+c.
    • The null hypothesis for case-control pair matched data is that the proportion of subjects exposed to the risk factor is equal for cases and controls pa+b = pa+c.



    Step 2. Calculate the value of the test statistic specific to H0

    The McNemar statistic, which is based on the chi-square distribution and is computed using the counts in the b and c cells of the contingency table, is given by the following equation:



    Cells b and c are called the discordant cells because they represent pairs with a difference and must be b + c> 25. Cells a and d are the concordant cells, do not contribute any information about a difference between pairs or over time, so they aren’t used to calculate the test statistic. (Note: Mathematically speaking, the McNemar's test is undefined when b =c =0)


    On continuity correction for small samples

    Some authors recommend a version of McNemar’s test with a correction for continuity in the case of small samples but this is controversial. Since software now makes exact binomial probability feasible this correction is no longer needed.



    Step 3. Compare the value of the test statistic to values from the χ2- distribution

    Compare the estimated McNemar statistic with the critical values of chi-square distribution for a=0.05 and df=1. If (which means that p-value<0.05) then H0 is rejected.



    Step 4. Interpretation

    Interpretation of the results.

The children were asked whether they knew how to manage their asthmatic episodes appropriately at both the start and completion of the camp (the results are shown to the below table). Was a significant change in children's knowledge of asthma management between the beginning and completion of the health camp (a=0.05)?
